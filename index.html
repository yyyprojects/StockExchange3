What is a Stock Exchange?

Imagine a massive, organized marketplace. Instead of selling fruits or antiques, this market is for buying and selling tiny pieces of ownership in huge companies like Apple, Disney, or Nike. That's a stock exchange. This section explains its two main purposes. Click on each card to learn more.

For Companies: Raising Money

Why would a company sell pieces of itself? To get money, known as capital, to grow their business.

By selling shares on a stock exchange, a company can raise large amounts of cash from the public. They can use this money to build new factories, hire more employees, develop new products, or expand into new countries—all without having to take out a loan from a bank.

For Investors: Growing Wealth

Why would you buy a tiny piece of a company? To share in its success and hopefully make a profit.

When you buy a company's stock, you become a shareholder. If the company does well and its value increases, the price of your stock may go up. You can then sell it for more than you paid. It's a way for ordinary people to invest their money and potentially grow their wealth over the long term by participating in the economy's growth.

The Major Leagues: NYSE vs. Nasdaq

In the USA, two exchanges dominate: the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) and the Nasdaq.
While they both do the same basic job, they have different histories, styles, and list different types of companies.

    The Referee: The SEC

    The stock market isn't a free-for-all. A powerful government agency, the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC), acts as the market's police officer. Its main job is to protect investors and maintain fair, orderly, and efficient markets. The SEC accomplishes this through three key functions, which you can explore in the tabs below.

    Fighting Fraud and Scams

    The SEC's primary role is to be your watchdog. It investigates tips and complaints about potential scams, such as companies lying about their products or financial health to trick people into buying their stock. If the SEC finds evidence of fraud, it can bring civil lawsuits against individuals and companies, resulting in hefty fines and other penalties to deter bad behavior.

    Preventing Unfair Advantages

    The SEC creates and enforces rules to ensure that no one has an unfair advantage. The most famous example is the prohibition of "insider trading." This rule prevents company executives, who have secret knowledge about an upcoming product or financial result, from using that information to buy or sell stock before the public knows about it. This helps level the playing field for all investors.

    Forcing Companies to Be Transparent

    To make informed decisions, investors need reliable information. The SEC mandates that all publicly traded companies must regularly disclose meaningful financial and other information to the public. This includes quarterly and annual reports detailing their sales, profits, debts, and potential risks. This transparency allows you to research a company and judge its health for yourself before investing.
